We're sampling people one at a time and updating our estimate of the average height on each side of the line as we go, shown in the bottom plot. The error bars represent uncertainty in estimating these averages. As we sample more and more people, our estimates settle down and our uncertainty decreases.

Believe it or not, after a few hundred samples we should have enough data to conclude that there's a statistically significant difference between heights on both sides of the line, no matter where we draw the line!

So far we have sampled 0 people and do not have statistical significance. This usually takes a few hundred points.

So what exactly does this mean?

Does it mean that there's a huge difference in heights on each side of the line?

Of course not!

All it means is that it's unlikely that we would see these results if the true distribution of heights was exactly the same on both sides of the line, so we declare statistical significance.

This is of course a bit of a silly, because we probably don't care whether things are exactly the same on both sides of the line, but this is what many statistical analyses are designed to test.

Instead, we probably care about how big the difference is between each side of the line, which is known as the effect size.

As you can see, there's a very small difference in average heights between both sides of the line. And of course not everyone is average.

In short, statistical significance doesn't mean practical significance.
